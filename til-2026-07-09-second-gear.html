Cheap labor creates work before it replaces jobs

Critical / Developer Tools The Pulse: Interesting AI coding stats from Cursor

Pre-read: AI coding is crossing from autocomplete into production delegation, and the bottleneck is shifting from generated text to operating discipline. Cursor says its developer habits report is based on aggregate product and engineering data across agent usage, accepted diffs, token use, and merged PR activity, which makes it one of the better windows into how heavy users actually behave. The important adjacent system is the software review pipeline: once agents can traverse repositories, edit many files, run tests, and prepare commits, the review step becomes a governance control rather than a productivity ritual. Cost control also starts to look like infrastructure, because agent sessions spend most of their budget reading and reusing codebase context before they write anything. The piece is worth opening because it puts numbers on a behavior change that many teams are feeling anecdotally.

Summary: Gergely Orosz summarizes Cursor data showing a steep usage divide: median users generate about 700 AI-assisted lines per week, while 90th percentile users generate closer to 9,000, and the top 1 percent reach roughly 30,000 to 40,000. The striking cost detail is that input and cached context dominate the economics; Cursor reports a roughly 10:1 input-to-output token pattern, with cache reads overwhelming raw output tokens once reuse is counted. Model economics are becoming more nuanced than sticker price: Opus 4.7 looks costly per agent request, but accepted-line economics narrow the gap against cheaper models. The most consequential datapoint is behavioral, not financial: the newsletter says the share of Cursor users letting AI changes reach commits without manual checking jumped from about 10 percent to around 40 percent in March 2026. That does not prove teams should trust agents blindly; it shows the pressure is now on review automation, diff risk scoring, context caching, and model-routing policies rather than on code generation alone.

Critical / Robotics What Would You Do if Labor Was Free?

Pre-read: Robotics progress matters most when it changes the price of physical work that currently never enters a budget. The near-term frontier is not a universal humanoid replacing a worker hour-for-hour; it is the conversion of neglected maintenance, cleaning, sorting, inspection, repair, and care-support tasks into explicit backlogs that machines can gradually attack. That framing fits the economics of general-purpose robots better than the job-replacement frame, because cheap capability expands demand for work that was previously uneconomic. The hard constraint is still reliability near messy human environments: recent reporting on humanoid deployments has focused on safety layers, insurance, and standards because embodied agents fail in ways that traditional industrial automation did not. Paxton's essay is useful because it asks what new work appears when cloud intelligence gets cheap hands.

Summary: Chris Paxton argues that radically cheaper robotic labor would expose a long tail of useful work that society currently leaves undone. His examples are concrete: preventative home and city maintenance, cleaner public spaces, automatically organized homes with searchable inventories, repair instead of disposal, custom-fit goods, fresh meal prep, and support tasks for teachers, childcare workers, and eldercare workers. The essay uses Jevons paradox to argue that cheaper labor can raise demand for labor-like services rather than simply reducing employment; more capability creates new projects, higher standards, and more decisions about what is worth doing. The most durable idea is that robotics may turn taste, prioritization, and supervision into higher-leverage human work while machines handle the physical execution. Paxton is more confident than the evidence fully permits about timing, but the strategic frame is strong: the winning robotics businesses may start by identifying large pools of valuable work that nobody currently buys because human hands are too expensive.

Happenings Data systems trade completeness for latency

Pre-read: Modern data architecture is usually a negotiation between when an answer is useful and when an input set is complete. Batch systems fit financial closes, daily reports, file imports, and retrospective analytics because they can wait for boundaries. Streaming systems fit alerts, personalization, fraud detection, logistics, and real-time operations because late certainty is less valuable than immediate action. The design pressure is visible across common tools: vendors such as Fivetran describe batch latency as inherent to periodic processing, while stream processors spend much of their complexity budget on event time, windows, late arrivals, and correctness.

Summary: ByteByteGo frames batch and streaming as two answers to the same question: when is incoming data complete enough to compute over? Batch waits for a natural boundary, a closed file, or a chosen interval, then processes the whole set or an incremental slice with high confidence. Streaming keeps computing while data is still arriving, which lowers latency but forces the system to estimate completeness and repair mistakes when delayed events appear. The article walks through the batch side with full loads, incremental loads, large-window aggregation, and micro-batches, then the streaming side with tumbling, sliding, and session windows, watermarks, late data, lambda and kappa architectures, and exactly-once semantics. The practical takeaway is that streaming is not simply faster batch processing. It is a different correctness model: every low-latency answer needs a policy for time, disorder, retries, deduplication, and revision.

Contextual Ideation Second Gear, Thursday, July 9

3 startup ideas from the Second Gear newsletter window.

Idea 1 Physical Work Backlog

A system of record for the maintenance, cleaning, inspection, and small repair work that buildings and cities postpone because labor is too expensive. The company starts as software plus managed execution: scan a property, turn neglected work into priced tasks, dispatch humans or robots where they already work, and learn which physical jobs become economical as robot capability improves.

Source Signals: What Would You Do if Labor Was Free? via It Can Think; China wants to solve the hardest problem in robotics - making hands via The Guardian; The Quest to Make Humanoid Robots Safe Enough for Humans via WSJ.

Why Now: Robots are getting cheap enough to make buyers ask what they would do with more physical labor, but autonomy is still too brittle for a pure robot-service company. The wedge is to inventory and price the work first, then route each task to the cheapest reliable execution path: human crew, teleoperated robot, narrow-purpose robot, or deferred automation.

First Wedge: Sell to regional property managers, school districts, and light-industrial campuses as a deferred-maintenance operating system: monthly site scans, task backlog, severity scoring, vendor dispatch, before/after evidence, and a budget forecast for what should be done before it becomes capex.

Commercial Model: Facilities and asset owners pay per site plus a take rate on executed work. The budget exists because deferred maintenance already leaks into insurance, tenant churn, failed inspections, emergency repairs, and ugly public spaces.

Defensibility: The compounding asset is a labeled map of small physical tasks, costs, failure rates, tools, surfaces, access constraints, and buyer willingness to pay. Robot companies will have hardware; this company owns the demand graph and the task library that tells which jobs are worth automating next.

Founder Fit: This is a robotics company disguised as a facilities workflow company. It requires taste in embodied AI, autonomy limits, sensor capture, task taxonomies, and where robots should enter before general-purpose humanoids are ready.

Technical Risk: The hard part is converting messy physical environments into reliable task specs without sending expensive humans to inspect everything. Computer vision will miss context, and early robot execution will be narrower than customers expect.

Market Expansion: Start with schools, apartments, and warehouses; expand to municipal assets, retail chains, hospitals, airports, solar farms, and insurance-driven property risk. The morphology is similar: assets decay, work is deferred, evidence matters, and small tasks are hard to schedule.

Self-Critique: This could collapse into ordinary facilities management software if the company does not build a real data loop into physical execution. It also depends on buyers caring about prevention before a crisis, which many do not.

Next Experiment: In two weeks, inspect 10 buildings with phone video, produce a ranked work backlog, get quotes from local vendors, and ask three property managers to pay for the report plus execution coordination. Track which tasks could plausibly be done by existing robots or teleoperation.

Idea 2 Embodied Change Control

A deployment gate for robots and physical AI agents: every new behavior, policy, model, route, and tool-use capability gets logged, simulated, risk-scored, approved, and watched in production like a code change. The primitive is not robot safety hardware; it is change management for machines that can touch the world.

Source Signals: Cursor Developer Habits Report: Agentic Coding Trends via Cursor; The Pulse: Interesting AI coding stats from Cursor via The Pragmatic Engineer; The Quest to Make Humanoid Robots Safe Enough for Humans via WSJ.

Why Now: Software teams are already letting agents land changes with less review. Robotics will face the same drift, except failed changes can damage inventory, hurt people, or void insurance. As robot fleets move from demos to customer sites, buyers will ask for proof that behavior changes are controlled.

First Wedge: Sell to warehouse robotics vendors and large operators as a robot-release control plane: connect to sim, logs, teleop review, incident tickets, geofences, and fleet rollout. Block high-risk behavior changes until they pass scenario tests and named operator approval.

Commercial Model: Robot vendors pay per fleet or per deployed robot because enterprise customers, insurers, and safety teams will make this a sales blocker. Large operators can also buy it directly when they run mixed fleets from multiple vendors.

Defensibility: The moat is the corpus of behavior diffs, near-miss traces, scenario tests, site maps, and approval outcomes across fleets. Incumbent robot companies can build local logs, but a neutral layer is more credible to customers, insurers, and regulators.

Founder Fit: It sits directly between autonomy, embodied AI, systems reliability, and hard-tech commercialization. It is a future-facing control plane for machines, not another generic AI governance dashboard.

Technical Risk: The system must normalize wildly different robot stacks and produce risk scores that operators trust. If it only stores logs after the fact, it is compliance theater; it has to catch bad rollouts before they touch the floor.

Market Expansion: After warehouse robots, expand to humanoids in factories, delivery robots, construction autonomy, agricultural machines, autonomous labs, drones, and eventually home robots. The common pattern is physical agents receiving frequent software and policy updates.

Self-Critique: This may be too early if robot fleets remain small and bespoke. Vendors may resist a neutral control layer unless customers or insurers force it into procurement.

Next Experiment: Interview 10 robotics deployment leads and 5 warehouse safety or insurance buyers. Build a thin adapter around one open robotics stack that records behavior diffs, runs a small scenario suite, and generates an approval packet a customer could understand.

Idea 3 Manipulation Skill Packs

A data and deployment company for narrow dexterous tasks that are too varied for traditional automation but too repetitive to ignore: refurbishing returns, repairing appliances, sorting odd parts, packing fragile goods, and prepping food. Each skill pack bundles task data, teleop demonstrations, tactile traces where available, evaluation scenes, and a robot-cell recipe that an operator can buy.

Source Signals: China wants to solve the hardest problem in robotics - making hands via The Guardian; What Would You Do if Labor Was Free? via It Can Think; Cursor Developer Habits Report: Agentic Coding Trends via Cursor.

Why Now: Dexterous hardware is no longer the only blocker. The scarce asset is task-specific manipulation data that includes how humans move, what they feel, and where the task fails. Returns, repair, and refurbishment centers already have concentrated examples and clear labor pain.

First Wedge: Start with one high-volume refurbisher or reverse-logistics operator. Build a teleoperated manipulation station for a single task, such as grading and opening returned electronics, removing batteries, or replacing worn components, then package the dataset and cell procedure for similar operators.

Commercial Model: Operators pay setup plus usage because every recovered item has measurable margin. Robot manufacturers and integrators can later license the skill packs to make their hardware useful in specific verticals.

Defensibility: Every deployment adds demonstrations, failure cases, object variants, tactile edge cases, and economic benchmarks. A generic humanoid vendor has broad demos; this company has the messy long-tail task data that makes a station pay back.

Founder Fit: This is embodied AI at the point where real businesses hurt: manipulation, data collection, autonomy evaluation, and hardware/software integration. It is technical enough to be interesting and commercial enough to test quickly.

Technical Risk: Generalizing from one operator's object stream to another may be harder than expected. Tactile sensing and teleop data may not transfer cleanly across robot hands, grippers, or fixture designs.

Market Expansion: Move from electronics returns to appliance repair, apparel rework, medical-device reprocessing, lab sample prep, food prep, and light manufacturing. The expansion path follows task morphology rather than industry labels.

Self-Critique: The company could become a systems integrator if it cannot standardize skill packs across customers. It also risks being squeezed if robot OEMs own both the hardware and the data loop.

Next Experiment: Pick one returns category, film 200 human task executions, label contact points and decision branches, then test whether a teleop robot cell can complete 20 percent of cases with positive unit economics. Sell the benchmark to one refurbisher before building autonomy.
