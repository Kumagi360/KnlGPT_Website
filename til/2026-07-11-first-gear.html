Critical / Iran Iran's Unseen Supreme Leader Becomes a Liability

Pre-read: Authoritarian succession depends on visibility as much as formal appointment. In the Islamic Republic, public religious authority, Revolutionary Guard backing, and crisis legitimacy have to reinforce one another, especially after a violent transfer of power. This Reuters piece is worth opening because it turns a rumor-prone absence into a map of institutional fragility.

Summary: Reuters reports that Mojtaba Khamenei, installed after the strike that killed his father, has remained almost completely out of public view, including during major funeral ceremonies. Senior sources say he was injured and disfigured in the strike but continues making decisions with Revolutionary Guard support. Ordinary Iranians are noticing the absence, and the optics are worsening as renewed U.S.-Iran tension raises the stakes for visible command. The newsletter also connects the succession question to President Trump's declaration that the ceasefire is over, demands around the Strait of Hormuz, and higher U.S. gasoline prices as fighting lifts oil. The deeper issue is regime signaling: a leader who cannot appear publicly may still govern, but his invisibility hands opponents, citizens, and foreign adversaries a simple test of strength.

Happenings Ukraine cannot quickly replace Patriot supply

Pre-read: Air defense is an industrial problem before it is a diplomatic promise. Patriot interceptors require specialized production, trained integration, and enough supply to defend cities while Russia keeps using ballistic missiles to stress Ukraine's limited inventory.

Summary: Reuters says Trump's pledge that Ukraine could produce Patriot air-defense missiles will take time to become operational, with experts saying production could take at least a year to start. Patriots remain uniquely important because they are the system Ukraine relies on against Russian ballistic missiles. Al Jazeera similarly frames the issue as licensing and production capacity rather than immediate battlefield relief. The practical implication is a vulnerable gap: Kyiv can receive political permission before it receives usable interceptors, leaving commanders to ration coverage across cities, infrastructure, and high-value military sites.

Happenings Spanish wildfire turned roads into traps

Pre-read: Evacuation failures often happen when official shelter-in-place guidance collides with panic and fast-moving fire behavior. Roads can become lethal when smoke, wind shifts, and traffic compress escape options.

Summary: Reuters reports that residents in rural Andalusian villages fled against advice from local mayors and police and drove into the path of a mountain wildfire. Four people died in one car, while eight others were found after apparently abandoning vehicles and trying to escape on foot. The episode is a grim systems lesson: emergency messaging, road design, and public trust decide whether mobility saves people or concentrates them in danger. It also sits inside a broader climate-risk pattern in which Mediterranean heat and drought are making fire behavior harder for local authorities to manage in real time.

Critical / AI Interpretability Reading Between Claude's Lines

Pre-read: AI labs are moving from output inspection toward tools that expose intermediate representations. The practical stakes are safety and control: if researchers can see what a model is preparing to say, they can test deception, manipulation, hidden goals, and eval-awareness before those behaviors reach the user. Anthropic's new work is worth opening because it gives that problem a concrete measurement surface.

Summary: Contrary Research paired two Claude stories: Anthropic's official Jacobian lens research, which identifies a small J-space of internal patterns connected to words Claude is likely to say later, and reporting that Claude Code used hidden prompt markers to flag suspected China-linked proxy or resale abuse. The interpretability result is the more durable item: it suggests that part of Claude's internal computation behaves like a reportable workspace, letting researchers observe concepts such as test-awareness or emotional valence before output. The Claude Code episode is a trust caution: The Decoder and other accounts describe timezone, proxy, and domain checks encoded into subtle prompt changes, while Anthropic staff characterized the feature as an anti-abuse experiment that was rolled back. Together, the stories show a frontier-lab tension that will keep recurring: the same privileged access that can improve safety can also become surveillance-like product behavior if it is hidden from developers.

Happenings Apple escalates OpenAI hardware fight

Pre-read: OpenAI's consumer-hardware push has turned talent migration into a strategic threat for Apple. The Jony Ive and ex-Apple hardware orbit makes the legal question unusually consequential because design, supply chain, and device-roadmap knowledge are hard to separate from employee expertise.

Summary: Reuters reports that Apple sued OpenAI and two former employees, alleging trade-secret misappropriation tied to OpenAI's consumer-hardware ambitions. The complaint says confidential information moved through former Apple staff and other channels; AP separately reports that Apple says OpenAI recruited employees and encouraged disclosure of confidential data. OpenAI denies seeking proprietary information. The litigation matters because Apple and OpenAI moved from distribution partners in iOS to possible hardware rivals, and the case may shape how aggressively AI labs can recruit from device companies while building ambient-computing products.

Happenings SpaceX's AI value is still terrestrial

Pre-read: Space infrastructure becomes financially legible when it can attach to terrestrial demand. The AI boom gives satellites, launch, power, and data-center adjacency a richer equity story than exploration alone.

Summary: Reuters reports that analysts see SpaceX's near-term AI payoff as tied to Earth rather than outer space. The company's narrative increasingly depends on infrastructure that supports AI demand, while more speculative space-compute visions remain longer dated. That framing matters for valuation: investors can underwrite launch cadence, connectivity, and AI infrastructure demand sooner than asteroid-mining or orbital-compute markets. The newsletter also notes SK Hynix's U.S.-listed shares rising in a Nasdaq debut and management warning of a severe memory shortage in 2027, reinforcing the same point from the chip side: AI capital spending is turning physical infrastructure into the bottleneck and the prize.
