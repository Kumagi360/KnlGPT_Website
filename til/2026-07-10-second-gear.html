Critical / Middle East Trump wants to leave the Iran war behind. That won't happen soon

Pre-read: The Strait of Hormuz remains the pressure point where Gulf security, oil flows, and U.S. credibility converge; even limited attacks can force Washington to defend bases, shipping, and allies at once. A ceasefire that leaves each side able to claim deterrence often buys time, but it rarely resolves command-and-control risk or the domestic politics of retreat. This Reuters piece is worth opening because it frames the war as an exit problem, not a battlefield update.

Summary: Reuters reports that President Trump's attempt to move past the Iran war has run into a fresh exchange of attacks after Iran targeted U.S. military sites in Bahrain and Kuwait, following U.S. strikes on Iranian targets tied to tanker attacks in the Strait of Hormuz. Trump said an interim deal to end the conflict was "over" and ordered new strikes, underscoring how quickly a face-saving off-ramp can collapse when both sides need to show resolve. The article's core point is that Trump's options are narrow and mostly bad: absorb future provocations and look weak, escalate and deepen a war he wants to leave, or accept a compromise that may not survive the next maritime or proxy attack. For the reader, the durable model is that Middle East wars become hardest to end when the political requirement for victory outlasts the military rationale for continuing.
